The Lovings

Richard and Mildred Loving

The Lovings were an ordinary family living in the state of Virginia during the 1950s. The only thing that stood out from this couple was that Richard was white and Mildred was of African American and Native American decent. This was against the law in 16 states at the time, but that did not stop them. The two had fallen in love and by the time Mildred became pregnant at the age 18, the two went off to get married. Knowing that it was against the law in Virginia, the two traveled to Washington D.C. to get married and then returned back to Virginia. Richard and Mildred saw nothing wrong with their marriage and family, but a lot of people did.

Violating the Law

When word caught wind in their hometown that Richard and Mildred had gotten married out of state the law enforcement took immediate action. When the two were questioned in their home about their marriage, the Lovings showed the sheriff their marriage certificate. What they did not know was that the law in Virginia did not allow for white and black people to marry in another state and return back to Virginia, which is exactly what they had done. To them it was the only way they could have married because of the Racial Integrity Act of 1924 that was present in Virginia. This act, also known as an anti-miscegenation law, prohibited any two people classified as "white" and "colored" from marrying one another. The Lovings were in a losing situation and Richard was taken away to the jail where he spent one night while Mildred had to spend several nights even though she was pregnant. Because of this the couple decided to plea guilty and were forced to leave Virginia and not able to return together for 25 years.

Loving v. Virginia

After trying to stay away from Virginia and family, Richard and Mildred moved back to Virginia in secret. They were tired of hiding and being kept from their families. The Lovings searched for help to take their case to court. The American Civil Liberties Union offered their help and provided them with one of their lawyers. The case was brought in front of the Virginia Supreme Court of Appeals. After the court did not overturn the original ruling,they brought the case to the Supreme Court. They argued that not allowing marriage between interracial couples was a violation of the Fourteenth Amendment since it was based solely on race. The court ruled on June 12, 1967 that the Racial Integrity Act was unconstitutional which created a domino effect in other states where the ban on interracial marriages were uplifted. In an interview Mildred said, "What happened, we really didn't intend for it to happen. What we wanted, we wanted to come home". In the end, the Lovings moved back home to continue their lives together and to expand their family.

Impact Today

Since the ruling in 1967, there has been in an increase in the number of interracial marriages in the the United States.
